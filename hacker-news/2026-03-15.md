**1M context is now generally available for Opus 4.6 and Sonnet 4.6**
- **Summary:** Anthropic has announced the general availability of 1-million-token context windows for its Opus 4.6 and Sonnet 4.6 models. The update includes standard pricing across the full window, removing the previous "long-context premium" and claiming high retrieval consistency without significant performance degradation at scale.
- **Discussion:** The thread is highly technical, focusing on the trade-offs between massive context windows and context compaction/caching. Users debated the "needle in a haystack" performance, with some reporting significant drops in coherence past 200k tokens, while others highlighted successful use cases in massive codebase refactoring and reverse engineering. There is significant discussion on the economics of KV caching and the potential shift from quadratic attention to more linear architectures or sparsity-based shortcuts to maintain standard pricing.

**Ageless Linux – Software for humans of indeterminate age**
- **Summary:** Ageless Linux is a Debian-based distribution designed as a form of "malicious compliance" against California's Age-Appropriate Design Code Act (AB 2273). The project refuses to implement age verification, instead declaring its software illegal to distribute in jurisdictions requiring such checks, effectively highlighting the conflict between privacy-preserving software and age-gating mandates.
- **Discussion:** The conversation centered on the legality and efficacy of age verification laws. Critics argued that such laws are technically infeasible for open-source software and harmful to privacy, while others viewed the project as a clever satirical protest. Technical insights included discussions on how "illegal to distribute" labels might impact mirror operators and the broader implications for decentralized software distribution in regulated jurisdictions.
- **Links:** [Article](https://agelesslinux.org/) | [HN Thread](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47381791)

**Montana passes Right to Compute act (2025)**
- **Summary:** Montana has enacted the "Right to Compute" act, which protects the rights of individuals and businesses to engage in digital asset mining and run high-performance computing hardware. The law prevents local governments from using zoning or discriminatory utility rates to target computing operations, effectively making Montana a haven for decentralized computing and crypto infrastructure.
- **Discussion:** The debate focused on the balance between state-level protections for industry and local environmental or noise concerns. Some commenters argued that the act favors large incumbents by creating barriers to entry disguised as "standards," while others praised it as a necessary defense against regulatory overreach targeting specific types of computation. There was also technical discussion on power grid stability and the heat-reuse potential of large-scale mining operations.

**Baochip-1x: What it is, why I'm doing it now and how it came about**
- **Summary:** Andrew "bunnie" Huang details the development of the Baochip-1x, a "mostly-open" 22nm SoC designed for high-assurance applications. The chip features a custom RISC-V core with a focus on inspectability, allowing users to verify the hardware design from the RTL level all the way to the software stack, addressing growing concerns about supply chain security and hardware backdoors.
- **Discussion:** The thread praised the project for pushing the boundaries of hardware transparency. Technical insights included discussions on the inclusion of a Memory Management Unit (MMU) in a microcontroller-class chip, which enables more robust process isolation. There was also significant interest in the "mostly-open" nature of the TSMC 22nm fabrication process and the challenges of creating truly open silicon in a proprietary foundry ecosystem.

**An ode to bzip**
- **Summary:** A technical deep dive into the bzip2 compression algorithm, praising its unique use of the Burrows-Wheeler Transform (BWT) instead of the more common LZ77. The article explores why bzip2 remains relevant for specific data types where context reordering provides better compression ratios than dictionary-based methods, despite being slower than modern alternatives like Zstd.
- **Discussion:** Discussion focused on the "physics" of compression, with experts comparing BWT to modern rANS (range Asymmetric Numeral Systems). Users shared benchmarks comparing `bzip2 -9` against `zstd -19`, noting that while Zstd often wins on speed and ratio, bzip2's lack of a large dictionary makes it superior for very small files or memory-constrained environments. There was also praise for the recently developed bzip3, which modernizes the algorithm for 10-50x speed improvements.
